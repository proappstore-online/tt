# TT — Competitor Research

> Market landscape for **personal & team time-tracking apps** as of 2025.
> Purpose: inform product decisions, identify differentiation, and locate market gaps TT can exploit.

---

## 1. Competitor Map

### Tier 1 — Market Leaders

#### Toggl Track
- **URL:** toggl.com/track
- **What it does:** Timer-based time tracking with projects, clients, tags, reports, and integrations (100+). Best-known brand in the space.
- **Pricing:** Free (up to 5 users, basic reports), Starter $10/user/mo, Premium $20/user/mo.
- **Platforms:** Web, macOS, Windows, iOS, Android, browser extension.
- **What users love:**
  - Dead-simple start/stop timer
  - Excellent reports with visual breakdowns
  - Cross-platform sync is rock-solid
  - Free tier is genuinely generous
  - Chrome extension for one-click tracking in other tools
- **What users hate:**
  - Reports locked behind paid plans
  - Overkill for solo users — too many settings
  - Mobile app is laggy / buggy compared to desktop
  - No AI features
  - Integrations are a selling point but often break
- **Market position:** Horizontal (everyone from freelancers to agencies)

---

#### Clockify
- **URL:** clockify.me
- **What it does:** Free-forever time tracking, unlimited users, projects, reports. Monetises with advanced features (scheduling, expenses, approval workflows).
- **Pricing:** Free (unlimited users!), Basic $4.99/user/mo up to Enterprise $14.99/user/mo.
- **Platforms:** Web, macOS, Windows, iOS, Android, browser extension.
- **What users love:**
  - Truly free with no user cap — hard to beat
  - Clean UI compared to competitors
  - Kiosk mode (for punch-in/out in physical locations)
  - Good CSV/PDF export
- **What users hate:**
  - Free tier lacks basic features users expect (e.g. rounding, targets)
  - Slow to ship new features
  - Weak mobile experience
  - Reports less intuitive than Toggl
  - No AI or smart features
- **Market position:** Budget/free tier dominator; competes on price

---

#### Harvest
- **URL:** getharvest.com
- **What it does:** Time tracking + invoicing + expense tracking. Strong focus on freelancers and agencies who bill clients.
- **Pricing:** Free (1 user, 2 projects), Pro $12/user/mo.
- **Platforms:** Web, macOS, Windows, iOS, Android.
- **What users love:**
  - Seamless time → invoice flow
  - Integrations with Asana, Jira, Basecamp, QuickBooks
  - Clean UX, feels professional
  - Forecasting companion app (resource planning)
- **What users hate:**
  - Expensive for teams
  - Overkill if you don't invoice
  - Reporting is basic vs. Toggl
  - Limited free tier (2 projects is very restrictive)
- **Market position:** Freelancer/agency billing tool that happens to track time

---

### Tier 2 — Strong Challengers

#### Timely
- **URL:** timelyapp.com
- **What it does:** AI-first automatic time tracking — logs everything you do (meetings, apps, documents) and lets you review/approve entries. No manual timer needed.
- **Pricing:** Starter $9/user/mo, Premium $16/user/mo, Unlimited $22/user/mo.
- **Platforms:** Web, macOS, Windows, iOS, Android.
- **What users love:**
  - Near-zero effort — tracks passively in the background
  - "Memory" timeline shows your whole day automatically
  - Great for people who forget to start timers
  - Clean, modern UI
- **What users hate:**
  - Privacy concerns (it records everything)
  - Expensive
  - Approval workflow can be clunky
  - Overkill for simple billing use cases
- **Market position:** Premium/AI segment; privacy-tolerant knowledge workers

---

#### RescueTime
- **URL:** rescuetime.com
- **What it does:** Automatic productivity tracking — measures time in every app/website and categorises it (productive vs distracting). Focus time tools.
- **Pricing:** Free (basic), Premium $12/mo.
- **Platforms:** Desktop daemon + web dashboard; iOS/Android.
- **What users love:**
  - Passive, zero-effort logging
  - Weekly reports are eye-opening ("you spent 3 hours on Twitter")
  - Focus sessions (blocks distracting sites)
  - Good long-term trend data
- **What users hate:**
  - Not billable-hours focused — can't assign to clients/projects
  - Categories are crude
  - Feels like surveillance, not empowerment
  - Reports have barely changed in years
- **Market position:** Personal productivity / digital wellness niche

---

#### Everhour
- **URL:** everhour.com
- **What it does:** Time tracking embedded directly inside project management tools (Asana, Trello, Jira, ClickUp, GitHub). Tracks against existing tasks.
- **Pricing:** Free (up to 5 users, limited), Team $10/user/mo.
- **Platforms:** Web + browser extension (primary); iOS/Android.
- **What users love:**
  - Tracks against actual tasks — no double-entry
  - Deep Asana integration is best-in-class
  - Budget tracking per project
  - Clean reports
- **What users hate:**
  - Useless without a supported PM tool
  - Limited standalone mode
  - Slow support
- **Market position:** Integration-first; teams already in Asana/Jira ecosystem

---

#### Trackabi
- **URL:** trackabi.com
- **What it does:** Time tracking + gamification + employee monitoring (screenshots, GPS). Unusual combo targeting micro-managing employers.
- **Pricing:** Free (3 users), plans from $3.99/user/mo.
- **What users love:**
  - Gamification (points, leaderboards) keeps employees engaged
  - Very affordable
- **What users hate:**
  - Screenshot/GPS monitoring feels invasive — employee backlash
  - UI is dated
- **Market position:** SMB monitoring niche

---

#### Timeular
- **URL:** timeular.com
- **What it does:** Time tracking with a physical 8-sided die — flip the die to a face = start tracking that project. Pairs with a software app.
- **Pricing:** $99 hardware + $9/mo subscription.
- **What users love:**
  - Physical interaction is tactile and memorable
  - Great for people who hate opening apps
  - Unique, gift-able product
- **What users hate:**
  - Expensive
  - You need the hardware on your desk always
  - Software alone is mediocre
- **Market position:** Hardware-differentiated novelty/premium niche

## 4. What Users Consistently Complain About (across all apps)

Pulled from App Store reviews, Reddit (r/freelance, r/productivity), ProductHunt, and G2/Capterra reviews:

1. **"Reports are locked behind a paywall"** — even basic weekly summaries require upgrading.
2. **"The mobile app is an afterthought"** — most were built web-first; iOS/Android feel bolted on.
3. **"Too many features I'll never use"** — Toggl, Clockify, and Harvest are feature-bloated.
4. **"I forget to start/stop the timer"** — manual discipline is the #1 failure mode.
5. **"I can't understand my own data"** — reports show numbers but no narrative interpretation.
6. **"Setting up projects and clients is tedious"** — friction at setup kills adoption.
7. **"No AI features despite being in 2025"** — users want smart summaries, pattern detection, suggestions.
8. **"Team reports feel like surveillance"** — employees resent time tracking imposed top-down.

## 5. Market Gaps & TT's Opportunity

### 🥇 Gap 1: AI-Native Time Intelligence
**The problem:** No mainstream time-tracker gives you a *narrative* understanding of your week. You get numbers and charts; you still have to interpret them yourself.

**TT's answer:** AI weekly digest (Pro) — natural language summary of work patterns, anomalies, and insights. This is something Toggl and Clockify explicitly don't have and would take them significant effort to ship.

**Why TT can win here:** `app.ai.chat()` makes this a one-feature implementation. Competitors would need to build or integrate an LLM pipeline.

---

### 🥈 Gap 2: Simplicity as a Feature
**The problem:** Toggl and Clockify have grown into sprawling products. New users are overwhelmed. The "just start a timer" use case is buried under integrations, workspaces, and settings.

**TT's answer:** Opinionated, fast, focused. One timer. Projects. Tags. Weekly view. AI digest. Nothing else until it's proven. The feature list is a selling point, not a limitation.

**Why TT can win here:** Incumbents can't un-build features. TT starts lean.

---

### 🥉 Gap 3: Knowledge Worker Personal Productivity (not billing)
**The problem:** Most time trackers are built for billing hours to clients. The language (clients, invoices, billable rate) alienates the huge segment of knowledge workers who want personal productivity insight, not invoicing.

**TT's answer:** Neutral language — "projects" and "tags," not "clients" and "billable hours." The AI digest is framed around *understanding yourself*, not billing a client. Target the "where did my week go?" user, not the "I need to invoice $X" user.

**Why TT can win here:** A clear persona shift. Toggl/Harvest are too invested in the billing narrative to rebrand. Clockify is neutral-ish but has no AI and is feature-bloated.

---

### 💡 Gap 4: Team Transparency Without Surveillance
**The problem:** Hubstaff and Trackabi offer team time tracking but with screenshots and GPS — employees hate it. Even Toggl team reports feel like employee monitoring.

**TT's answer:** Team reports that are opt-in by employees; framed as "shared project visibility" not monitoring. The owner/moderator sees aggregate data, not per-minute surveillance.

**Why TT can win here:** Culture is shifting against surveillance software. A team tool that employees actually *want* to use is differentiated.

## 7. What TT Should NOT Try to Compete On

| Area | Why to avoid |
|------|-------------|
| **Native mobile apps** | Toggl/Clockify have 5-star native apps; web-only is a constraint to acknowledge, not fight |
| **100+ integrations** | Integration platforms (Zapier) exist; build them later if users ask |
| **Invoicing** | Harvest owns this with deep QuickBooks integration; it's a different product |
| **Passive auto-tracking** | Timely owns this and requires desktop daemons — out of scope for a web app |
| **Lowest price** | Clockify's free-unlimited tier can't be undercut; compete on value, not price |
